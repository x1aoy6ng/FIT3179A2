Malaysia's Unemployment

Unemployment rate is a key indicator of a country's economic performance and labour market health. It reflects the proportion of the labour force that is not currently employed and available for work, regardless of whether they are actively seeking employment. The latest labour report released by the Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM) shows that the unemployment rate stood at 3.0% in August 2025, signaling a stable job market as Malaysia continues to strengthen its post-pandemic recovery.

Unemployment Rate by State in Malaysia

The map illustrates the unemployment rate across Malaysian states. From 2016 to 2020, Sabah consistently appears in darker shades, indicating one of the highest unemployment rates in the country. This ongoing pattern points to challenges in the state's labour market, such as limited industrial diversification and fewer opportunities for high-value employment.

Youth Unemployment Trends in Malaysia

The chart illustrates youth unemployment trends in Malaysia from 2016 to 2020, focusing on two key age groups: 15–24 and 25–30 years old. Overall, youth unemployment shows a steady upward movement over the five-year period.

The total number of unemployed youths rose from about 4,762 in 2016 to over 6,000 in 2020, with a noticeable spike in the final year. This sharp increase likely reflects the economic slowdown and job market challenges triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic, which disproportionately affected young job seekers entering or navigating the workforce.

Unemployment Rate Distribution by Gender and Stratum

Unemployment is fairly evenly distributed between males and females, indicating that both face similar challenges in the job market. However, the slightly higher share among women suggests possible inequalities in job access, participation, and representation across industries.

The chart compares unemployment between urban and rural areas. A larger proportion is observed in urban regions, which often attract more job seekers due to better access to education, industries, and services. In contrast, rural areas record a smaller share of unemployment, though the issue there can be more deep-rooted, tied to limited industrial activity and reliance on primary sectors.

Unemployment Duration Distribution

The chart shows how unemployment duration varies throughout the year, illustrating the share of individuals unemployed for different periods — three months, six months, twelve months, and more than twelve months. Overall, most unemployed individuals secure work within the first three to six months, while long-term unemployment remains relatively low. However, a gradual increase in longer durations toward the end of the year may signal slower job recovery or reduced hiring activity during that period.

Active vs Inactive Unemployment Trends

Active unemployment represents individuals who are actively seeking work but have not yet secured a job.

Inactive unemployment refers to those who are not currently looking for work, possibly due to discouragement or temporary withdrawal from the labour force.

The chart shows a steady increase in active unemployment from January, peaking towards the end of the year, reflecting greater job-seeking activity amid challenging economic conditions. In contrast, inactive unemployment fluctuates, with a sharp rise around April — likely linked to pandemic-related disruptions — before gradually declining as job opportunities began to recover.
