Malaysia's Unemployment Rate

The latest Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM) report shows that Malaysia’s unemployment rate reached 3.0% in August 2025.

Click the year selector below to explore changes over time

Unemployment Rate by State

Sabah records as one of the highest unemployment levels in Malaysia, shown by its consistently darker shading from 2016 to 2020.

This is largely due to its slower economic diversification, limited industrial development, and fewer opportunities for high-skilled employment compared to more urbanised states.

In contrast, most Peninsular states maintain relatively lower rates, suggesting more stable employment conditions and broader economic activity.

Youth Unemployment Trends

Over the five-year period, the total number of unemployed youths increased from about 4,762 in 2016 to over 6,000 in 2020.

The 15–24 age group consistently recorded the highest unemployment levels, reflecting early career challenges faced by school leavers and fresh graduates entering the job market.

The sharp rise in 2020 reflects the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic

Unemployment Rate Distribution by Gender and Stratum

Unemployment rates remain fairly balanced between males and females, indicating similar challenges in securing employment.

However, women experience a slightly higher rate, hinting at persistent inequalities in access to job opportunities and workforce participation.

Urban areas show a higher concentration of unemployment, as more job seekers pursue city opportunities.

In contrast, rural unemployment reflects limited economic diversification and fewer avenues for high-skilled work.

Unemployment Duration Distribution

Unemployment duration in Malaysia shows distinct patterns across different time frames: 3 months, 6 months, 12 months, and more than 12 months.

Most of the job seekers manage to find employment within the first three to six months, while longer-term unemployment (beyond a year) remains comparatively low.

Active vs Inactive Unemployment Trends

Active unemployment refers to individuals actively seeking work but yet to secure employment.

Inactive unemployment refers to people not seeking work, often due to discouragement or temporary withdrawal.

This pattern highlights how the pandemic disrupted labour participation and increased job-seeking pressure later in the year.
